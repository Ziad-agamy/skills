# Copywriting Frameworks Reference

These three frameworks underpin the entire landing page architecture. Reference them when building sections or when the user needs copy direction beyond the section formulas.

---

## 1. PAS Framework (Problem → Agitate → Solve)

**What it does:** Moves the reader from intellectual awareness of a problem to emotional urgency — and then positions the product as the only logical exit.

**When to use:** Section 3 (Problem/Agitation). Can also be woven into the Hero headline when the audience has high problem awareness.

### The Three Phases

**Phase 1 — Problem: Call it out with surgical precision**
Name the specific, deep-rooted problem. The more precise, the better. Avoid category-level pain ("managing a team is hard") and go to the lived experience level ("you spend Sunday nights dreading the Monday status call because nothing moved forward last week").

Precision signals that you understand the problem from the inside. Generic problem statements feel like a company guessing. Specific ones feel like a friend who's been there.

**Phase 2 — Agitate: Make the reader feel the weight of the problem**
This is where most landing pages go soft. Don't soften it. Amplify the pain by exploring:
- What it costs them (time, money, relationships, sleep)
- What they've already tried that didn't work — and why those failures stung
- The compounding effect: what happens if this problem continues for 6 more months? A year?
- The emotional dimension: embarrassment, frustration, feeling stuck, watching others succeed

The goal is not to be cruel — it's to make the status quo feel genuinely uncomfortable, so the solution feels like genuine relief.

**Phase 3 — Solve: Present the product as the obvious way out**
After the agitation, the reader is primed. Don't restate features. Present the product as the specific solution to the specific pain you've been describing. The pivot should feel like a hand reaching down to help someone out of a hole.

Transition phrases: "That's exactly why we built [Product]." / "There is a better way." / "Introducing [Product] — the only [category] built specifically for [audience] who [pain]."

### Example
```
Problem:
You're sending cold emails every day. You've tried different subject lines, 
different angles, different times of day. Your reply rate is 2%.

Agitate:
That means 98 out of every 100 people you reached out to ignored you completely. 
And while you're spending 3 hours a day on outreach, your competitors are closing 
deals. You've read the threads. You've bought the courses. And yet here you are — 
staring at an inbox that doesn't move. This isn't a hustle problem. You're working 
hard. The old approach just doesn't work anymore.

Solve:
ReplyFlow is the only cold email tool built around verified buying signals — 
so you only reach out to prospects who are actively looking for what you sell, 
right now.
```

## 2. Transformation Framework

**What it does:** Structures the Hero headline and sub-headline to communicate the full value proposition within 5 seconds.

**When to use:** Section 1 (Hero) — headline and sub-headline. Also useful for the Section 8 (Final CTA) headline.

### The Three Layers

**Layer 1 — Big Idea:** The overarching concept or category of the offer. What world is the visitor entering?

**Layer 2 — End Result:** The tangible, specific outcome the user will achieve. Not vague ("more success") — specific ("close 40% more deals", "lose 15kg in 12 weeks", "cut design time in half").

**Layer 3 — Transformation:** The before/after shift. Who are they now vs. who they become after using this? This is the emotional layer — it speaks to identity, not just outcome.

### How to Construct the Headline

The headline should collapse all three layers into a single powerful statement. You don't need to use all three explicitly — they should be felt, not spelled out.

**Formula options:**
- `[End Result] for [Audience] [Without Pain/Constraint]`
- `The [Adjective] Way to [Dream Outcome]`
- `Finally, [Dream Outcome] — Without [Common Frustration]`
- `[Transformation phrase]: [Specific Result] in [Timeframe]`

### Example
```
Big Idea: Project management software for remote teams
End Result: Ships projects on time, no more deadline chaos
Transformation: From a team that misses every deadline → to one that ships predictably

Headline: "Ship Every Project on Time — Without the Sunday-Night Panic"
Sub-headline: "The project management tool built for remote teams who are done 
with missed deadlines and endless Slack check-ins."
```

### Three Diagnostic Questions
If stuck on a headline, answer these and combine:
1. What's the product or offer?
2. What do you promise it will do?
3. What makes it so special?

The answer to #2 + #3 combined almost always produces a strong headline.

## 3. Scanning Architecture Framework

**What it does:** Optimizes the entire page for the reality that visitors only read about 20% of the content on a page. The page must communicate its message even to people who never read a full sentence.

**When to use:** Applied to the whole page as a design and copy principle. Reference this when advising on layout, emphasis, and visual hierarchy for any section.

### The Three Phases

**Phase 1 — Headline Dominance**
Every section must have a headline that communicates the full value of that section on its own. A visitor who only reads the headlines of every section should come away with the complete story: problem, solution, proof, offer, objection-handling.

Test: Strip out all body copy and sub-bullets. Do the headlines alone tell a coherent story? If not, rewrite them until they do.

**Phase 2 — Emphasis**
Use visual emphasis (bold, color, size) to draw the eye to the 20% of body copy that matters most:
- Key benefit statements in bold
- Transformation words underlined or colored
- CTA buttons in high-contrast colors that break the visual flow
- Numbers and specific results always bolded ("Save **5 hours per week**" not "save five hours per week")

Never bold randomly. Only bold what a scanner absolutely must read.

**Phase 3 — Visual Hierarchy**
Guide the eye from the most important to the least important:
```
LARGEST: Section headline (the core message)
  ↓
MEDIUM: Sub-headline or supporting statement
  ↓
SMALL: Body copy (the detail for readers who engage)
  ↓
ACCENT: Bolded proof points or statistics within the body
```

Every section should have this hierarchy. A flat section where every element is the same size signals to the brain that nothing matters — so the brain reads nothing.

### The Scanning Test
After writing any section, ask: "If someone spent 3 seconds on this section, what would they take away?" If the answer is "nothing specific," rewrite the headline and bold the key sentence in the body.
